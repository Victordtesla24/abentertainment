| P0       | Delivery Planning     | In `Proposed Tech Stack & Architecture` and `Implementation Roadmap`, the blueprint commits to Next.js 15, Sanity, Clerk, Stripe, LangGraph, n8n, sponsor analytics, heavy motion, PWA, multilingual support, and multiple AI agents inside a 14-week delivery window.                                                              | This is a classic scope-stack mismatch: the number of integrations, editorial workflows, QA surfaces, and operational dependencies materially exceeds the stated schedule and budget, which makes slippage, partial launches, or quality regressions highly likely.                                        | Re-scope to a phased MVP: launch with core marketing pages, Sanity, event pages, contact/inquiry flow, and manual editorial controls; defer chatbot, sponsor portal, social automation, PWA, multilingual support, and advanced animation until after production telemetry validates the base platform.                    |
| P0       | Booking Flow          | In `Data Flow & Security`, `Functional Requirements`, and `Implementation Roadmap`, the booking design jumps from client form to Stripe checkout to webhook completion without defining an order model, booking states, deposit rules, cancellation/refund flows, or reconciliation logic.                                          | Payment systems fail at the edges, not the demo path. Without an explicit order lifecycle, idempotent webhook handling, and post-payment reconciliation, the platform can create duplicate bookings, mismatched payment states, and manual finance/support cleanup.                                        | Introduce a Postgres-backed booking/order state machine with explicit statuses (`initiated`, `pending_payment`, `paid`, `failed`, `refunded`, `cancelled`), Stripe idempotency keys, webhook-first fulfillment, refund/admin workflows, and reconciliation reports before calling the payment flow production-ready.       |
| P0       | Privacy & Compliance  | In `Data Flow & Security`, `AI Concierge Chatbot (24/7)`, `Sponsor Management Portal`, and `Email marketing automation`, the compliance framing is reduced to “GDPR/Privacy” plus cookie consent, while the system collects attendee, sponsor, and lead data in Australia and reuses it for AI, marketing, analytics, and outreach. | This misses the real compliance surface: Australian Privacy Principles, secondary-use controls, direct marketing consent, retention, deletion, and notifiable data breach obligations. A privacy banner does not cover multi-system customer profiling or AI enrichment.                                   | Replace the GDPR-only framing with an APP-aligned privacy architecture: define collection purposes, consent capture, direct-marketing rules, retention schedules, data subject workflows, vendor data map, breach response/NDB runbook, and prohibited AI reuse cases for attendee and sponsor data.                       |
| P0       | AI Architecture       | In `Fully Automated AI Workflows` and `Additional AI Agents`, the blueprint assumes AI generation, classification, and publishing can run as linear happy-path pipelines without durable checkpoints, dead-letter handling, model/prompt versioning, moderation, or explicit human approval gates.                                  | These workflows directly affect public content, SEO, email, sponsor outputs, and analytics. Without persistence and review controls, transient vendor errors, hallucinated outputs, or duplicate triggers can silently publish bad content or create operational churn that the team cannot audit.         | Add a production workflow contract: persistent job state, LangGraph checkpointer, n8n retry plus dead-letter queue, model/prompt version registry, publish approvals for public-facing outputs, automated validation rules, and cost/error dashboards per workflow.                                                        |
| P1       | Performance           | In `Vision for the New Experience`, `Animations & Interactions`, and `Non-Functional Requirements`, the blueprint combines autoplay hero video, WebGL, GSAP, Framer Motion, magnetic cursor behavior, and large galleries while promising mobile Lighthouse 98+ and LCP under 1.2 seconds.                                          | Those claims are not impossible, but they are not credible as default outcomes for a media-heavy luxury site unless strict performance budgets drive design choices from the start. Right now the document promises both maximal motion and maximal mobile performance without acknowledging the tradeoff. | Define explicit performance budgets by route and asset class, make advanced effects conditional enhancements, default the homepage hero to an optimized poster/image on mobile, and move WebGL and cursor effects out of launch scope unless profiling on representative devices proves they fit the budget.               |
| P1       | CMS Model             | In `Sanity Schema for Events` and `Fully Automated AI Workflows`, the content model covers basic event fields but omits editorial workflow states, release scheduling, locale variants, audit metadata, asset rights, consent artifacts, rollback fields, and operational fields required for automation safety.                    | A minimal document schema is not enough when AI agents, scheduled publishing, sponsor content, SEO metadata, and future multilingual content all depend on predictable lifecycle states. Missing governance fields force brittle logic into workflows and make recovery harder when data is incomplete.    | Expand the schema and governance model to include workflow status, publish/review roles, release scheduling, locale strategy, asset rights/usage fields, provenance for AI-generated content, audit timestamps, rollback-ready snapshots, and validation that blocks incomplete records from automated downstream actions. |
| P1       | Authorization         | In `Data Flow & Security` and `Sponsor Management Portal`, the blueprint treats Clerk as a complete answer for access control but never defines sponsor tenant isolation, admin override boundaries, invite/offboarding flow, or which analytics a sponsor may legitimately see.                                                    | Authentication proves identity; it does not define what each organization can read or change. Without a role matrix and tenant-scoped data rules, the sponsor portal risks data leakage, incorrect analytics exposure, and unreviewable privilege creep.                                                   | Define an authorization matrix for sponsor users, AB admins, and operators; scope all sponsor data queries by organization; add audit logs for access and content changes; document invite/offboarding flows; and treat analytics as signed, reproducible outputs rather than ad hoc dashboard numbers.                    |
| P1       | SEO                   | In `Upcoming Events Automation`, `Accessibility & SEO`, and `Content & Brand Voice`, the blueprint proposes AI-generated schema, metadata, blogs, OG assets, and “instant indexing” through Google Indexing API for event content.                                                                                                  | This overreaches in two ways: the Google Indexing API is not for general event-page indexing, and fully automated SEO content without editorial QA increases the risk of inaccurate structured data, thin pages, and spam-like content patterns that can damage search trust.                              | Replace “instant indexing” with sitemap/Search Console submission and structured-data validation, restrict automated schema generation to validated fields only, and require editorial approval for AI-generated copy on event, blog, and sponsor pages before publication.                                                |
| P1       | Budgeting             | In `Estimated Budget Ranges` and `ROI Projection`, the budget and operating-cost model materially undercounts AI inference, image/video processing, storage/CDN egress, monitoring, support labor, content review, and premium-tier product features.                                                                               | This creates a board-facing planning risk: the blueprint makes the project look cheaper to build and run than it is, which increases the chance of mid-project scope cuts, under-supported launches, or sponsor/client disappointment when recurring costs appear later.                                   | Replace the top-line estimate with a bottom-up cost model that separates implementation, launch support, recurring SaaS/platform costs, AI usage ceilings, storage/egress, moderation/review labor, and a contingency buffer of at least 20–30% for integration and content operations.                                    |
| P1       | Operations            | In `Risk Mitigation` and `Launch & Monitoring`, operational readiness is reduced to weekly reviews and a short post-launch observation window, with no queue monitoring, alert thresholds, runbooks, or owner model for AI and integration failures.                                                                                | Multi-vendor systems fail asynchronously. A chatbot, email pipeline, Stripe webhook flow, or social queue can degrade without the public site failing visibly, and the current document does not explain who notices first, how they respond, or when to roll back.                                        | Add an operational readiness section covering SLOs, dashboards, alerting, queue depth/error-rate thresholds, vendor outage playbooks, content freeze/rollback procedures, moderation incident handling, and named ownership for each automation surface.                                                                   |
| P2       | Accessibility         | In `Accessibility & SEO` and `Signature interactions`, the document shifts between WCAG 2.2 AA and AAA while simultaneously adding parallax, motion-heavy transitions, cursor effects, autoplay media, and cinematic lightboxes.                                                                                                    | The accessibility ambition is good, but the requirements are internally inconsistent. A launch target of “target AAA” with luxury-motion patterns will either be watered down later or create design churn when reduced-motion, keyboard, and screen-reader paths are added belatedly.                     | Set WCAG 2.2 AA as the formal launch bar, define reduced-motion and keyboard-first behavior for every advanced interaction, require captions/transcripts for video, and treat AAA improvements as a tracked post-launch enhancement set rather than a blanket promise.                                                     |
| P2       | Migration             | In `Implementation Roadmap` and `Launch & Monitoring`, WordPress content migration is allocated two days and is described as a straightforward port, despite the current site containing duplicate content, placeholders, broken images, and SEO debt.                                                                              | Migration is not only data transfer; it is content triage, redirect mapping, metadata parity, asset cleanup, and acceptance testing. Underestimating it risks broken URLs, lost organic traffic, and shipping the same content quality issues into the new stack.                                          | Add a migration workstream with content inventory, redirect-map validation, metadata/slug parity checks, image QA, legal/rights review for reused media, a content freeze window, and at least one rehearsal migration before production cutover.                                                                          |
| P2       | Analytics             | In `Sponsor Management Portal` and `ROI Projection`, the blueprint promises real-time sponsor analytics and aggressive conversion uplift without defining event taxonomy, attribution rules, consent-aware measurement, or confidence bounds for ROI claims.                                                                        | Analytics promises become credibility problems when stakeholders ask how “impressions,” “attribution,” or uplift were calculated. Without a measurement plan, sponsor dashboards and ROI narratives can become persuasive-looking but non-auditable outputs.                                               | Define a measurement specification before implementation: event taxonomy, attribution windows, consent-aware analytics boundaries, source-of-truth dashboards, sponsor metric definitions, and confidence ranges for ROI projections instead of single-number promises.                                                    |
| P2       | Conversational UX     | In `AI Concierge Chatbot (24/7)`, the assistant is expected to answer event queries, initiate bookings, capture sponsor leads, recommend events, handle multilingual responses, and escalate complaints/refunds, but the escalation and support model is underspecified.                                                            | This creates a service-design gap: the bot can collect sensitive information or make commitments without a defined handoff SLA, supported-language quality standard, or boundaries for when it must stop and defer to humans.                                                                              | Narrow the launch chatbot scope to high-confidence FAQs and routing, add retrieval confidence thresholds, create explicit escalation intents for complaints/refunds/sponsor requests, define supported languages and fallback behavior, and log every handoff for operational review.                                      |
| P2       | Media Governance      | In `Photo Gallery Intelligence`, `Graphics & Visuals`, and `Additional AI Agents`, the blueprint assumes AI enhancement, categorization, and highlight generation across event photos and videos without rights metadata, retention rules, or content-safety review.                                                                | Live-event media often includes performers, audience members, minors, and sponsor marks. Publishing or transforming those assets without rights and sensitivity controls exposes the business to brand, legal, and community trust risks.                                                                  | Add asset-rights metadata, consent/release policy, retention and archival rules, moderation gates for public publishing, and storage/egress budgets for image and video derivatives before scaling AI media workflows.                                                                                                     |
| P3       | Prompt & Code Quality | In `Ready-to-Use Code Snippets & Prompt Templates`, the snippets are framed as production-ready despite missing validation layers, JSON schema enforcement, accessibility/performance fallback strategy, and security/error-handling context.                                                                                       | Readers may mistake illustrative code and prompts for implementation-ready assets, which can seed fragile patterns directly into delivery. That is especially risky in a repository that currently contains planning artifacts only.                                                                       | Reframe the snippets as reference examples, add “not production complete” caveats, and pair each snippet with the missing guardrails it requires in real delivery: validation, tests, fallbacks, security boundaries, and operational error handling.                                                                      |
| P3       | Scope Discipline      | In `Functional Requirements`, `Vision for the New Experience`, and `Implementation Roadmap`, lower-value features such as dark mode, PWA offline support, multilingual expansion, and deep animation polish sit too close to launch-critical work in the same narrative.                                                            | Even when tagged as lower priority, including them in the same flagship blueprint encourages stakeholder expectation creep and makes it harder to defend a disciplined launch cut line once execution begins.                                                                                              | Recast the blueprint into explicit launch, post-launch, and experimental tracks, and make the launch track intentionally boring where needed: events, inquiries, content publishing, analytics baseline, and stable payments before experiential extras.                                                                   |
